the Sierpinski Triangle

  • Sierpinski Triangle The formation of the Sierpinski triangle begins with a single filled triangle. With each iteration, the area of each remaining filled triangle is reduced by 1/4 as the middle interior equilateral triangle is removed. We observe that there are 3^n filled triangles on the nth iteration, each with 1/4 the area of the filled triangles of the previous iteration. Thus the limit of the Sierpinski triangle's area is zero.
  • Chaos Game The Chaos game gives a method for generating arbitrarily close approximations to the Sierpinski triangle. It can be described with the following algorithm:

    1 Begin with three vertices and randomly choose a point X inside the triangle they form.

    2 Randomly choose one of the three vertices and draw an imaginary line between X and our random vertex.

    3 Plot a dot on the midpoint Y of this line.

    4 Let X=Y and go to step 2. Repeat an arbitrarily large number of times.
  • Sierpinski Curve The Sierpinski arrowhead curve is a base-motif fractal identical to the Sierpinski triangle for arbitrarily large numbers of iterations. Notice that the length of the one-dimensional curve that forms this fractal is increased by a factor of 3/2 with each iteration. The Sierpinski triangle-- also called the Sierpinski gasket or Sierpinski sieve-- is a self-similar fractal appearing in 13th century Italian art with its formal description attributed to Polish mathematician Waclaw Sierpinski in 1915. Its Hausdorff dimension is log(3)/log(2)~1.5848 and its area is zero. In this page, we use interactive animations to examine the Sierpinski triangle, a stochastic method for generating arbitrarily close approximations to it, and an identical fractal built with a one-dimensional curve.
